# What I Talk About When I Talk About Running

**Author:** Haruki Murakami  

**Finished:** 2025-11-05
**Theme:** `Running` · `Writing` · `Discipline` · `Solitude`

> Long repetition can turn an ordinary act into a private form of thinking.

</div>

---

## Core Reflection

*What I Talk About When I Talk About Running* is a memoir about running, but also a quiet account of writing, solitude, endurance, and self-knowledge. Murakami does not present running as a metaphor forced onto life. He simply shows how a repeated physical practice becomes part of a writer’s way of being.

The book’s central standard is inward rather than outward. The point is not whether someone else is impressed, but whether one has improved over yesterday and met the standards one has set for oneself.

---

## Repetition and Self-Respect

One highlighted note captures the book beautifully: keeping at a mundane action long enough can make it contemplative. Ten minutes of reading, one daily run, one writing session, one repeated practice may look ordinary in isolation. But when placed inside years of repetition, it becomes a life pattern.

Murakami’s running is not about spectacle. It is about continuity. Long-distance running fits him because the opponent is the former self. The measure is not public validation, but whether the practice can be sustained honestly.

---

## Solitude as a Strength

The book is also unusually comfortable with solitude. Murakami writes about not needing to talk or listen while running, about not being suited to team sports, and about having a quiet inner motivation. He does not turn this into a universal prescription. He simply accepts it as part of his temperament.

That acceptance is one of the book’s most humane qualities. Independence has a cost. Emotional hurt may be part of not living entirely through other people’s approval. But the book suggests that a person’s particular temperament can also become an asset when understood and used well.

---

## Writing and Running

Murakami’s discipline as a runner mirrors his discipline as a novelist. He stops writing while he can still continue, returns the next day, and trusts the accumulation. Writing a novel, like running a marathon, requires total commitment and a willingness to stay with discomfort without dramatizing it.

The book is not loud about ambition. It is about the quieter ambition of building a life around work one can keep doing.

---

## Takeaway

The strongest lesson of the book is that endurance is personal. It does not always look heroic from the outside. Sometimes it looks like going out for another run, writing another page, or repeating one small practice for years.

A sustained life is built less from occasional intensity than from a steady relationship with one’s own standards.
