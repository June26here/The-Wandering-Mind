# A Brief History of Intelligence

### Evolution, AI, and the Five Breakthroughs That Made Our Brains

**Author:** Max Bennett

**Finished:** 2026-06-19

**Theme:** `Evolution` · `Neuroscience` · `AI` · `Human Intelligence`

> How did intelligence happen the first time, and what can that tell us about the future of artificial intelligence?

</div>

---

## Core Reflection

The future of silicon-based intelligence remains uncertain. There are many possible paths toward artificial superintelligence, and it is still unclear which one, if any, will truly work. Yet *A Brief History of Intelligence* suggests that before trying to recreate intelligence, it may be necessary to understand how intelligence emerged in the first place.

Max Bennett frames the evolution of intelligence as a sequence of five major breakthroughs: **steering, reinforcement learning, simulating, mentalizing, and speaking**. This structure makes intelligence feel less like a sudden miracle and more like a long chain of biological inventions, each one built on top of earlier layers.

Human intelligence, in this view, is not separate from life. It is life, redirected and refined across time.

---

## Intelligence Begins With Action

One of the most striking ideas in the book is that intelligence begins with movement. Early organisms did not become intelligent in the abstract. They had to sense, move, approach, avoid, hunt, and survive.

The first form of intelligence was not philosophy or language. It was the ability to decide:

- move toward what helps
- move away from what harms
- adjust based on feedback

This makes intelligence feel deeply practical. Action creates feedback, feedback creates learning, and learning creates more adaptive behavior. Trial and error is therefore not a primitive failure mode. It is one of the foundations of intelligence.

This also offers a useful lens for human learning. Growth often does not begin with perfect understanding. It begins with trying, receiving feedback, and updating the next move.

---

## From Trial and Error to Inner Simulation

The book’s discussion of reinforcement learning connects biology and AI in a particularly elegant way. Dopamine is not simply about pleasure. It is about pursuit, prediction, and reinforcement. Wanting, learning, habit, and motivation are all part of the machinery that makes intelligence useful in the real world.

But the next leap is even more important: learning before acting.

With the neocortex, mammals gained the ability to simulate possible actions before performing them. This means intelligence is no longer limited to direct trial and error. A brain can build an inner model of the world, test possible futures, and choose before moving.

This is where the book becomes especially relevant to AI. Large language models can predict language impressively well, but human intelligence is not only language prediction. It also depends on world models, causal reasoning, bodily experience, and an inner simulation of reality.

A system that predicts words may appear intelligent. But without a richer model of the world, something essential may still be missing.

---

## Intelligence Is Social

Another major step is **mentalizing**: the ability to model other minds.

To understand others, humans imagine what another person sees, knows, wants, or misunderstands. This ability likely evolved for social life, but later became crucial for imitation, teaching, cooperation, and culture.

This makes intelligence less individual than it first appears. Human intelligence is not only the power of one brain solving problems. It is also the power of many minds modeling one another, sharing discoveries, correcting mistakes, and accumulating knowledge.

Language turns private simulations into shared ones. A thought can be compressed, transmitted, received, and rebuilt inside another mind. Reading is one of the most powerful forms of this transfer: it lets one person enter another person’s structured thinking across time.

---

## Culture as Collective Intelligence

The book also helps explain why human culture compounds. Humans cooperate through shared abstractions: money, countries, companies, laws, open-source communities, scientific fields, and moral rules.

These abstractions are not “fake” simply because they are imagined. They are coordination technologies. They allow strangers to act together at a scale no single brain could manage alone.

But cooperation also creates the problem of free riders. If information sharing is valuable, then communities also need norms, rules, and sometimes punishment to protect contribution. In this sense, law, intellectual property, and open-source governance can all be seen as modern versions of an ancient problem: how to keep collective intelligence alive without letting cooperation collapse.

---

## Back to Artificial Intelligence

The book’s final implication is not only technical, but also moral.

If artificial superintelligence becomes the next major breakthrough, then intelligence may shift from a biological medium to a digital one. But the central question is not only whether humanity can build such intelligence. It is also what kind of intelligence should be built, and what values should be carried into it.

*A Brief History of Intelligence* makes the future of AI feel less like a clean break from biology and more like the next chapter in a much older story. Human intelligence emerged through action, feedback, simulation, social understanding, and shared language. If artificial intelligence is to become something deeper than today’s models, it may need more than scale. It may need its own versions of world models, agency, social understanding, and value alignment.

---

## Takeaway

The most powerful idea in this book is continuity.

Human intelligence did not arrive from nowhere. It was assembled slowly, through ancient biological tools that were reused, extended, and recombined. The same may be true of artificial intelligence. The future of AI may not be a rejection of human intelligence, but a mirror of the evolutionary path that created it.

The story of intelligence is not finished. It may still be at the beginning.
